# Cerenkov Light Images of EAS Produced by Primary Gamma Rays and by Nuclei

## Citation

- Author: A. M. Hillas.
- Title: "Cerenkov Light Images of EAS Produced by Primary Gamma Rays and by Nuclei".
- Publication: 19th International Cosmic Ray Conference, Vol. 3.
- Report number: OG-9.5-3.
- NASA NTRS Document ID: 19850026666.
- Local PDF: `raw/nasa/ntrs-19850026666/19850026666.pdf`.

## Source type

Foundational IACT / Cherenkov image morphology conference paper. This source is used to establish the Hillas-parameter style description of Cherenkov images and the early gamma/hadron separation logic.

## Key extracted claims

1. The paper argues that very effectively beamed background hadronic showers and TeV gamma-ray showers from a point source can be distinguished using the width, length and orientation of Cerenkov light images in a focusing mirror focal plane.
2. The paper compares computer simulations of air showers with early Whipple Observatory observations and reports agreement in image behavior.
3. The detector context is the Whipple Observatory at Mt. Hopkins, with a 10 m reflector and a focal-plane photomultiplier camera used to record image details.
4. A typical simulated gamma-ray image is elongated along a line pointing back toward the source position in the camera plane.
5. The paper defines image parameters that later became associated with Hillas-style image analysis: LENGTH, WIDTH, MISS, AZWIDTH, DISTANCE, and FRAC(2).
6. LENGTH and WIDTH describe the weighted angular spread along and across the major axis of the image.
7. MISS is the perpendicular distance from the source position to the image axis; DISTANCE measures the distance from image centroid to source position; FRAC(2) records the concentration of signal in the two largest tubes.
8. A selection based on image-parameter domains can reject a large fraction of proton background while retaining most gamma-ray showers.
9. The source reports an example selection requiring at least 4 of 6 image parameters to lie in the gamma domain, rejecting roughly 60–70% of proton background while retaining most gamma-ray events.
10. The paper notes that higher-energy showers appear farther from the source position in the camera and implies that larger field of view becomes important at higher energies.
