# Broadband observations of the naked-eye gamma-ray burst GRB 080319B

## Source identity

- Authors: J. L. Racusin et al.
- Journal: Nature 455, 183-188 (2008).
- DOI: 10.1038/nature07270.
- arXiv: 0805.1557.
- Local PDF: `raw/arxiv/0805.1557/0805.1557.pdf`.
- arXiv source package: not available from the attempted e-print endpoint in this session; the response was another PDF copy, saved as `raw/arxiv/0805.1557/0805.1557-eprint-response.pdf` to avoid mislabeling it as TeX source.

## Extracted key claims

- GRB 080319B is the “naked-eye” GRB; the optical emission reached about visual magnitude 5.3.
- The burst redshift is z ≈ 0.937.
- Prompt optical and gamma-ray light curves were temporally related, but the optical flux was far above a simple low-energy extrapolation of the gamma-ray spectrum.
- The prompt optical and gamma-ray emission therefore require distinct spectral components in the source interpretation, rather than one simple synchrotron component.
- The outflow is interpreted as extremely relativistic.
- The afterglow behavior is interpreted with a two-component / narrow-core plus wide-jet geometry.
- In the source interpretation, a very narrow core with opening angle about 0.2° is viewed close to its axis and is associated with the prompt extreme brightness.
- In the source interpretation, a wider component with opening angle about 4° contributes to the later afterglow.
- The afterglow modeling favors a wind-like environment consistent with a massive-star origin for long GRBs.

## Figure / evidence candidates

- Prompt optical and gamma-ray light curve comparison: supports temporal relation but spectral mismatch.
- Broadband afterglow light curve and model decomposition: supports the narrow-core / wide-component interpretation.
- Schematic or model figure for viewing geometry: useful for explaining why on-axis viewing of a narrow core produces an extreme apparent burst.

## Caveats

- The two-component / narrow-core plus wide-jet geometry is a model interpretation, not a direct image of the outflow.
- Opening angles, Lorentz factors, density, and energy partition are model-dependent and depend on assumed microphysics and circumburst density profile.
- The optical/gamma-ray mismatch rules out a simple one-component spectral extrapolation but does not uniquely determine the emission mechanism.
