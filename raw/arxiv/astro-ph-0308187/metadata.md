## Extracted key claims

- GRB 030329 is treated as a nearby event at z = 0.1685, enabling radio calorimetry.
- The source reports centimetre radio observations beginning about 13.8 hours after the burst, with millimetre and sub-millimetre observations from the broader dataset.
- The observed radio afterglow was the brightest radio afterglow detected to date in this source's context, but its 8.5 GHz peak luminosity was typical of other long-duration GRBs because of the low redshift.
- The radio decline at t ≳ 10 d and decreasing peak flux below about 22.5 GHz are interpreted as evidence for a collimated explosion.
- Radio modeling requires a jet break at t_j,rad ≈ 9.8 d, corresponding to θ_j,rad ≈ 0.3 rad or 17°.
- For the radio component, the beaming-corrected kinetic energy is E_K ≈ 2.5×10^50 erg, comparable to other modeled afterglows.
- Optical and X-ray observations show an early break at t ≈ 0.55 d; the source models this early component as a narrow jet with opening angle about 0.09 rad or 5°.
- The optical resurgence around 1.5 d requires a second emitting component in this source's interpretation.
- The source's two-component model assigns gamma-ray emission plus early optical/X-ray afterglow to the narrow 5° ultra-relativistic component, and radio afterglow plus late optical emission to the wider 17° mildly relativistic component.
- The beaming-corrected gamma-ray energy emitted by the narrow jet is E_gamma ≈ 5×10^49 erg, below the then-discussed clustering around 1.3×10^51 erg.
- The source argues that the wide, mildly relativistic component carries most of the energy, so the total explosive yield can be similar to other GRBs even when the ultra-relativistic gamma-ray output is low.

## Figure candidates

- `f1.ps`: radio light curves and uniform / wind synchrotron models; includes t_j ≈ 10 d and θ_j ≈ 0.3.
- `f2.ps`: radio-to-X-ray light curves, early optical t_j,opt ≈ 0.55 d, optical rise at t ≲ 1.5 d attributed to the slower second jet component, and combined SN + two jet components.
- `f3.ps`: histograms comparing beaming-corrected gamma-ray energy, X-ray-inferred kinetic energy, and total relativistic energy.

## Caveats

- The two-component assignment is a model interpretation by Berger et al.; the observational inputs are the radio light curves, optical/X-ray break, optical resurgence, and SN 2003dh contamination/subtraction issue.
- The source explicitly notes residual discrepancies in the two-component fit and the need for accurate photometry plus careful SN 2003dh subtraction.
- Some optical data referenced through GCN circulars were preliminary in the figure caption; avoid treating them as final high-weight photometry without later source checks.
